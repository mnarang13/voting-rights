The Voting Rights Act of 1965

The Voting Rights Act, a landmark piece of federal civil rights legislation, was signed into law by Lyndon B. Johnson in 1965 to ensure that state and local governments do not impose discriminatory voting policies. The Act secured the right to vote for racial minorities throughout the US, especially in the South. It contains multiple ways to regulate elections, including provisions that outlaw literacy tests and other methods that governments had historically used to disenfranchise minorities. Section 4(b) contains a "coverage formula" that determines which state and local governments are subject to the special provisions in Section 5, which include requiring federal approval, or "preclearance", before changing their election laws. The formula covered districts which had a history of racially discriminatory voting policies, and mainly targeted the Deep South.

In 2013, the Supreme Court voted 5-4 to invalidate Section 4 of the Voting Rights Act in Shelby County v. Holder. This decision made the provisions in Section 5 unenforceable and allowed several states to impose restrictive election laws without advance federal approval.

Restrictive Voting Measures

Photo ID laws, voter registration restrictions, voter purges, lack of polling places, felony disenfrachisement, and gerrymandering are among the many ways that politicians restrict voting. These measures disporportionally target people of color, students, the elderly, and people with disabilities.
